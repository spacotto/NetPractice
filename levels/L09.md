## Theoretical Concepts

### Route Summarisation (Supernetting)

In this level, the Internet router uses a `/22` destination route (`[e].198.[f].0/22`) to send traffic back to your `/25` local network. This is known as route summarisation or supernetting. Instead of the Internet maintaining thousands of individual entries for every small `/25` or `/24` network in the world, it groups contiguous subnets together into a single, larger overarching prefix (like a `/22`). This drastically reduces the size of global routing tables and improves hardware efficiency.

### Asymmetric and Fallback Routing

Level 9 demonstrates that a router does not need a perfectly matched specific route for every single subnet if a fallback exists. The Internet router is configured to send `10.0.0.0/27` traffic back to Router 1. However, Host C lives at `10.0.0.254`, which is far outside that `/27` block. Despite this, communication still succeeds because the Internet router falls back on its `default` route (`Ir3`) when it cannot find a specific match, ensuring the packet still finds its way back to your internal gateway.
