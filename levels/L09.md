## How to Solve Level 9

###  Goal 1: host **meson** needs to communicate with host **ion**

To solve Goal 1, you only need to **establish local Layer 2 communication** between `Host A` (meson) and `Host B` (ion). Because they are both plugged into the **same switch** (neutron), they do not need a router to talk to each other; they just need to be on the exact same subnet.

By default, their interfaces are on completely different network boundaries:
* `A1` is on a `/24` network (`192.168.[g].2` with mask `255.255.255.0`).
* `B1` is on a `/16` network (`192.168.[h].42` with mask `255.255.0.0`).

Because their subnet masks and dynamic third octets (`[g]` vs `[h]`) do not match, they drop each other's packets. Thus, you just need to **align their IPs and masks** so they share the exact same Network ID.

However, before you just slap a `/24` on both of them, look at `Router R1` (proton). `Interface R11` is also plugged into that same switch, and its subnet mask is permanently locked to `255.255.255.128` (`/25`): **align the entire switch to that locked mask now**.

In summary:
* **Set Subnet Masks:** Change the mask on both `A1` and `B1` to `255.255.255.128`.
* **Align Network IDs:** Pick a number for the third octet and make sure `A1`, `B1`, and `R11` all share it. For example, if you make them all `192.168.42.x`.
* **Verify Host IPs:** Ensure the final octet for `A1` and `B1` falls into the same `/25` block (e.g., between `.1` and `.126`). The defaults of `.2` and `.42` are perfectly fine.

## Theoretical Concepts

### Route Summarisation (Supernetting)

In this level, the Internet router uses a `/22` destination route (`[e].198.[f].0/22`) to send traffic back to your `/25` local network. This is known as route summarisation or supernetting. Instead of the Internet maintaining thousands of individual entries for every small `/25` or `/24` network in the world, it groups contiguous subnets together into a single, larger overarching prefix (like a `/22`). This drastically reduces the size of global routing tables and improves hardware efficiency.

### Asymmetric and Fallback Routing

Level 9 demonstrates that a router does not need a perfectly matched specific route for every single subnet if a fallback exists. The Internet router is configured to send `10.0.0.0/27` traffic back to Router 1. However, Host C lives at `10.0.0.254`, which is far outside that `/27` block. Despite this, communication still succeeds because the Internet router falls back on its `default` route (`Ir3`) when it cannot find a specific match, ensuring the packet still finds its way back to your internal gateway.
